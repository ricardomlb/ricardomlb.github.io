This paper revisits the theoretical relationship between fertility decline and economic growth. 
To that end, we develop an endogenous growth model that combines fishing-out effects in innovation and a fixed cost of family formation. 
This setup identifies two economic drivers of lower fertility with asymmetric implications for long-run economic growth. 
When the cost of having an extra child increases, families substitute quality for quantity of children, resulting in faster economic growth. 
By contrast, when the fixed cost of having a family increases, fertility rates decline without a compensation of human capital, resulting in slower innovation and lower economic growth. 
The model thus predicts that fertility trends are not predictive of future economic growth.
